Kiranjyot Gill

Harvard Astrophysicist

A Little About Me...

I am a pioneering Sikh woman who has devoted over a decade to researching gravitational wave astrophysics. This January 2025, I will become the first Sikh graduate from Harvard, earning a PhD in Astronomy & Astrophysics. As the youngest member of the LIGO-Virgo Collaboration (now the International Gravitational Wave Network; IGWN), I was part of the global team that discovered gravitational waves, a breakthrough that earned us the 2017 Nobel Prize in Physics. My contributions have been recognized with several prestigious awards, including the 2016 Special Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics, the Princess of Asturias Award from His Majesty the King of Spain, the UK Royal Astronomical Society 2017 Group Achievement Award in Astronomy, and the Bruno Rossi Prize in High Energy Astrophysics from the American Astronomical Society. I have co-authored over 100 scientific publications with more than 9000 citations and delivered over 30 invited talks at universities, government summits, and media events. My research focuses on the groundbreaking detection of gravitational waves (GWs) from core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe) using terrestrial, space-based, and lunar-based interferometers. The successful detection of GWs from a CCSN would mark the second major milestone in the emerging field of GW astronomy and astrophysics.

Research Interests

My primary research interest lies in the detection of gravitational waves from core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe). This involves using a variety of interferometers including terrestrial, space-based, and lunar-based detectors. The successful detection of these waves would mark a significant milestone in the field of gravitational wave astronomy, similar to the initial discovery of gravitational waves. Additionally, I am involved in the study of binary black hole mergers, neutron star mergers, and the development of advanced data analysis techniques for gravitational wave detection.

Outreach

I am committed to engaging with the community and sharing the excitement of astrophysics and gravitational wave research. I have given numerous talks at schools, universities, and public events to inspire the next generation of scientists. I also work on outreach programs aimed at increasing diversity in STEM fields, with a focus on supporting women and underrepresented minorities. By communicating the importance and wonder of astrophysics, I hope to encourage more young people to pursue careers in science.
